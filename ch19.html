1 · Basics of Taxation

Every tax has two parts: a tax base (the measure on which the tax is levied — income, sales, property, profits) and a tax rate structure (the percentage of the base paid in taxes). Tax bases may be stock variables (property value at a point in time) or flow variables (income earned per month).

Types of Tax Rate Structures

Proportional Tax
Same percentage of income at every income level. A flat 20% income tax with no deductions.
Progressive Tax
Higher-income households pay a larger percentage. U.S. federal income tax — marginal rates rise with income (10% → 39.6% in 2014 brackets).
Regressive Tax
Higher-income households pay a smaller percentage. Payroll tax (capped base) and retail sales tax are regressive — lower-income earners spend a larger share of income on taxable goods.

Average vs. Marginal Tax Rates

Average Tax Rate
ATR = Total Tax Paid / Total Income
Describes what fraction of income overall goes to taxes.
Marginal Tax Rate
MTR = Tax on the Next Dollar Earned
Relevant for work/leisure and save/consume decisions. In a progressive system, MTR > ATR.
2014 U.S. Example (Single Filer, Standard Deduction)
At $100K income (taxable $93,800): total tax ≈ $19,441 → ATR ≈ 19.4%, MTR = 28%.
At $200K income (taxable $193,800): total tax ≈ $47,814 → ATR ≈ 23.9%, MTR = 33%.
When deciding whether to work extra hours, use the marginal rate — that's the tax on the next dollar earned.
Which Rate Matters for Decisions?
Choosing whether to work more hours → marginal rate. Evaluating how much of your income goes to taxes overall → average rate. Deciding whether to make a deductible donation → marginal rate (higher-income taxpayers get a bigger deduction benefit).

2 · Tax Incidence: Who Really Pays?

Tax incidence is the ultimate distribution of a tax's burden — who actually bears the economic cost, regardless of who sends the cheque to the government. Tax shifting occurs when households or firms alter behaviour to avoid paying a tax, shifting the burden to others.

A retail sales tax levied at the same rate on all consumer goods is hard to shift because demand for all consumer goods as a category is relatively inelastic. Narrow excise taxes on specific goods (artichokes) are easier to shift because consumers switch to substitutes.

3 · Incidence of Payroll Taxes

Social insurance payroll taxes made up 34.8% of federal receipts in 2014. Though initially levied on employers, the burden is typically shifted partly to workers. Here's the mechanism:

  1. A tax of $T per unit of labour increases the employer's cost of labour by $T.
  2. Firms reduce labour demand (substitute capital, cut output) → excess supply of labour.
  3. Wages fall, shifting part of the tax burden onto workers.
  4. Equilibrium: workers take home W₁ < W₀; firms pay W₁ + T.
Figure 19.3 — Payroll Tax Incidence
The tax of $T drives a wedge between what firms pay (W₁+T) and what workers take home (W₁). Both parties share the burden.

Elasticity and Who Bears the Burden

The burden-sharing depends on the elasticities of labour supply and demand:

  • Inelastic labour supply (workers keep working regardless of wage): workers bear most of the burden. Empirical evidence suggests U.S. labour supply is close to inelastic → workers bear most of U.S. payroll tax.
  • Elastic labour supply: workers reduce work significantly as wages fall, so firms can't lower wages much → firms bear more.
  • The split is the same whether the tax is initially levied on employers or employees — only the story differs.
Regressivity of the Payroll Tax
The U.S. payroll tax is regressive at higher incomes for two reasons: (1) wages and salaries form a smaller share of total income for high earners (who also earn dividends, profits, rents); and (2) the base was capped at $118,500 in 2015.

4 · Incidence of the Corporate Profits Tax

The corporate profits tax is a tax on the profits of incorporated firms. Who bears this tax?

  1. Tax initially reduces after-tax profits in the corporate sector.
  2. Capital shifts to the noncorporate sector (higher after-tax returns there).
  3. More competition in noncorporate sector → profits fall there too.
  4. Equilibrium: after-tax profit rates equalise across sectors.

Harberger (1962) concluded that owners of all capital — corporate and noncorporate — bear the corporate tax burden roughly in proportion to profits. Labour and consumer price effects are relatively small. Exception: for a monopolist, the corporate tax is not shifted — it falls entirely on the monopolist's profits, because a profit-maximising monopolist won't change price or output when a fixed fraction of profit is taken.

5 · Excess Burden and the Principle of Neutrality

When taxes distort economic decisions, they impose a burden on society that exceeds the revenue collected. The excess burden (also called deadweight loss) is this extra cost: the value destroyed by the tax-induced change in behaviour, which neither government nor taxpayer captures.

Total Burden of a Tax
Total Burden = Tax Revenue Collected + Excess Burden (DWL)
Figure 19.5 — Excess Burden of a Distorting Excise Tax
The shaded triangle (DWL) is excess burden — consumer surplus lost to neither government revenue nor consumers. The rectangle is tax revenue collected.

Principle of Neutrality

All else equal, neutral taxes — those that minimise distortion of economic decisions — are preferred. The size of excess burden grows with elasticity: the more responsive buyers/sellers are to the price change, the larger the deadweight loss. A perfectly inelastic demand curve produces zero excess burden (no behaviour change).

Why Land Taxes Are Efficient
Land is in perfectly inelastic supply — it can't move or be produced in response to a tax. A uniform land tax imposes almost no excess burden, making it one of the most efficient possible taxes.

6 · The Principle of Second Best

A distorting tax doesn't always create excess burden. The principle of second best says: if other distortions already exist in the economy, introducing a new distortion may actually improve overall efficiency.

Two cases where distorting taxes are desirable:

  1. Externalities: A Pigouvian tax on a polluting firm distorts output decisions — intentionally. The distortion corrects a prior inefficiency (uncorrected externality). No excess burden; instead, there is an efficiency gain.
  2. Pre-existing taxes: If Y and Z are already taxed at 5%, consumers shift excessively to X. Taxing X at 5% too reduces this distortion. Equal taxes on all goods prevent tax-avoidance switching.

7 · Tax Equity

Benefits-Received Principle

Taxpayers should pay in proportion to the benefits they receive from public expenditures. Example: fuel taxes funding highway construction. Difficult to apply for pure public goods (national defense), where benefits are collective and indivisible.

Ability-to-Pay Principle

Tax burdens should reflect ability to pay, independent of benefits received. Dominates U.S. tax policy.

Horizontal and Vertical Equity

Horizontal Equity
Those with equal ability to pay should bear equal tax burdens.
Vertical Equity
Those with greater ability to pay should pay more. Supports progressive taxation.

What Is the Best Tax Base?

Definition — Economic Income
Economic Income = Consumption + Change in Net Worth
Broader than IRS income: includes unrealised capital gains, employer benefits, imputed rent from owner-occupied housing, gifts.

Three candidate tax bases:

  1. Income: Current U.S. approach. Critique (Fisher): income taxes double-tax savings (once when earned, again on interest). Also distorts consumption/saving choice.
  2. Consumption: VAT systems. Argument: taxes what you "take out of the common pot." Avoids double-taxation of savings. Promotes saving and capital formation.
  3. Wealth/Net Worth: Estate taxes, property taxes. Argument: accumulated wealth better reflects true economic power. Critique: double-taxes already-taxed income if applied alongside income tax.
Net Worth
Net Worth = Total Assets − Total Liabilities

8 · Social Choice

Government exists to provide what "society wants" — but aggregating individual preferences into collective decisions is not straightforward.

Arrow's Impossibility Theorem (1951)

Kenneth Arrow proved that no voting system can simultaneously satisfy all reasonable axioms of fairness and always produce consistent, non-arbitrary results. Some social decision-making system will always violate at least one desirable criterion.

The Voting Paradox

Even with only 3 voters and 3 options, majority-rule voting can produce intransitive results (A beats B, B beats C, but C beats A). This shows that the agenda — the order in which options are voted on — can determine the outcome, giving enormous power to whoever sets the agenda (e.g., rules committees in Congress).

Logrolling

Representatives trade votes — D supports E's program in exchange for E's support of D's program. Logrolling can help pass programs that benefit small groups intensely while harming many slightly, but can also help efficient legislation pass.

9 · Key Terms

Tax Base
The measure on which a tax is levied (income, property, sales).
Tax Rate Structure
The percentage of the tax base that must be paid.
Proportional Tax
Same % of income at all income levels.
Progressive Tax
Higher % from higher-income households.
Regressive Tax
Lower % from higher-income households.
Marginal Tax Rate
Tax on the next dollar of income earned.
Tax Incidence
Ultimate distribution of the tax burden.
Tax Shifting
Altering behaviour to move the tax burden to others.
Excess Burden (DWL)
Burden of a tax beyond revenue collected — value destroyed by distortion.
Principle of Neutrality
Neutral taxes (minimal distortion) preferred, all else equal.
Principle of Second Best
A distorting tax can improve efficiency if other distortions already exist.
Benefits-Received Principle
Pay taxes proportional to benefits received from public spending.
Ability-to-Pay Principle
Tax burdens proportional to ability to pay, regardless of benefits.
Horizontal Equity
Equal tax burdens for those with equal ability to pay.
Vertical Equity
Greater tax burdens for those with greater ability to pay.
Economic Income
Consumption + change in net worth. Broader than taxable income.
Impossibility Theorem
Arrow: no voting system always yields consistent, non-arbitrary results.
Voting Paradox
Majority rule can produce intransitive preferences (A>B, B>C, C>A).
