1 · Gross Domestic Product: Definition

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is the total market value of all final goods and services produced within a given period by factors of production located within a country.

GDP is "the economic report card of a nation" — the single most important summary measure of an economy's output. U.S. GDP in 2014 was $17,418.9 billion.

The national income and product accounts, compiled by the Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA), have been called one of the great inventions of the 20th century. Before them, policymakers navigated the Great Depression using only stock price indices, freight car loadings, and incomplete industrial production indices.

GDP vs. GNP
GDP counts output produced within a country's borders, regardless of who owns the factors. GNP counts output produced by a country's citizens, regardless of where production occurs.

Honda plant in Ohio: profits count in Japanese GNP (Japanese capital) but wages count in U.S. GNP (U.S. labour). Both wages and profits count in U.S. GDP (produced within U.S. borders). U.S. GNP in 2014: $17,630.6B vs. GDP: $17,418.9B — a small difference.

2 · What GDP Does and Doesn't Count

Final vs. Intermediate Goods — No Double Counting

Intermediate goods are produced by one firm for use by another firm in further production. They are excluded from GDP because their value is already embedded in the price of the final good. Counting them would lead to double counting.

Value Added — Gasoline Example (Table 21.1)
StageValue of SalesValue Added
Oil Drilling$3.00$3.00
Refining$3.30$0.30
Shipping$3.60$0.30

Sum of values at all stages = $13.90, but GDP only counts the final retail value = $4.00 = sum of value added at each stage. Summing total sales would overstate GDP by $9.90.

What GDP Excludes

  • Used goods: Selling a used car transfers ownership — no new production. The car was counted in GDP when first made.
  • Paper transactions: Buying stocks, bonds, or other financial assets involves no current production. Exception: brokerage fees are counted (a service is being provided).
  • Output produced abroad by domestically owned factors: U.S. citizens working for a foreign company abroad → not in U.S. GDP. Foreigners working in the U.S. → counted in U.S. GDP.
  • Transfer payments: Social Security, welfare — no goods or services produced in exchange.
  • Government interest payments: Treated as transfers, not payments for current production.
GDP Relationship to Sales
GDP = Final Sales + Change in Business Inventories
GDP measures production, not just sales. Unsold goods that enter inventory are counted as investment (production for later sale).

3 · Expenditure Approach: C + I + G + NX

The expenditure approach calculates GDP by summing what all four sectors spend on final goods and services:

GDP — Expenditure Approach
GDP = C + I + G + (EX − IM)
C = Personal Consumption · I = Gross Private Domestic Investment · G = Government Consumption & Gross Investment · EX = Exports · IM = Imports
U.S. GDP Components, 2014 — Expenditure Approach
Consumption is by far the largest component at 68.5% of GDP. Net exports were negative in 2014 (imports exceeded exports).

C — Personal Consumption Expenditures (68.5% of GDP, 2014)

Durable Goods (7.5%)
Last a relatively long time: automobiles, furniture, appliances.
Nondurable Goods (15.3%)
Used up fairly quickly: food, clothing, gasoline, cigarettes.
Services (45.7%)
No physical item produced: legal, medical, educational services. Nearly half of GDP.

I — Gross Private Domestic Investment (16.4% of GDP)

Investment in economics = purchase of new capital — NOT stocks or bonds.

Nonresidential Investment (12.7%)
Machines, tools, plant equipment bought by firms.
Residential Investment (3.2%)
New houses and apartment buildings built by households and firms.
Change in Business Inventories (0.5%)
Goods produced now but intended for future sale. Positive = inventories grew; negative = inventories fell. Treated as investment because inventories are capital.
Gross vs. Net Investment
Net Investment = Gross Investment − Depreciation
Depreciation = fall in asset value each period. Net investment shows whether the capital stock is growing (positive) or shrinking (negative).
Capital stock at end of period = Capital at start + Net Investment

G — Government Consumption and Gross Investment (18.2% of GDP)

Includes purchases of final goods (bombs, pencils, school buildings) and services (military salaries, teachers). Does NOT include: transfer payments (Social Security, welfare) or government interest payments — neither represents payment for current production. Federal: 7.0% of GDP; State & Local: 11.2%.

NX — Net Exports (−3.1% of GDP, 2014)

Net Exports
NX = Exports (EX) − Imports (IM)
C+I+G includes spending on foreign goods (imports) → subtract IM. It misses goods sold abroad (exports) → add EX. In 2014: EX=$2,337B, IM=$2,875.2B → NX=−$538.2B (trade deficit).

5 · From GDP to Disposable Personal Income

The chain of income measures (2014 data):

The Income Chain: GDP → Disposable Personal Income
Disposable personal income = what households actually have to spend or save. Personal saving rate = personal saving / disposable income.
Personal Saving Rate (2014)
Personal Saving Rate = Personal Saving / Disposable Personal Income = 4.8%
Saving rates rise in recessions (anxiety about the future) and fall in booms (pent-up demand). In 2005 the rate reached a low of 2.5%.

6 · Nominal vs. Real GDP

Nominal GDP is GDP measured in current (today's) prices. If the price of pizza rises from $1 to $1.10 but production stays at 100 slices, nominal GDP rises from $100 to $110 — but no more was produced. Nominal GDP conflates price changes with output changes.

Real GDP is nominal GDP adjusted for price changes — it measures changes in actual production, holding prices constant. It is the better measure of economic output over time.

The Fixed-Weight Problem and the BEA's Solution

Using a single base year's prices to weight quantities (the old method) produced results that depended heavily on which year was chosen as the base. Growth rates varied wildly (24.8% vs 4.3% in the textbook example, depending on base year).

The BEA's current approach (chain-weighted real GDP):

  1. Compute growth using Year 1 prices as weights → get one growth rate.
  2. Compute growth using Year 2 prices as weights → get a second growth rate.
  3. Take the geometric average of the two growth rates.
  4. Roll forward: use Years 2 & 3 for the next period's calculation, etc.

This "splits the difference" between base years, producing more stable and accurate growth estimates.

Three-Good Economy Example (Table 21.6)
Two years, three goods (A, B, C). Nominal GDP: Year 1 = $12.10, Year 2 = $19.20 (+58.7%). But using Year 1 prices: real growth = 24.8%. Using Year 2 prices: real growth = 4.3%. BEA geometric average ≈ 14.09% — the chain-weighted real GDP growth rate.

7 · The GDP Deflator

While real GDP measures quantity, the GDP deflator measures the overall price level. It is defined as:

GDP Deflator
GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100
The deflator equals 100 in the base year. A deflator of 110 means prices are 10% higher than in the base year.

The GDP deflator differs from the Consumer Price Index (CPI): the deflator covers all final goods and services in the economy (including investment goods and government purchases), while the CPI tracks a fixed basket of consumer goods.

8 · Limitations of GDP as a Measure of Well-Being

GDP is an excellent measure of economic production, but a poor measure of well-being or quality of life:

  • Non-market activities ignored: Household production (cooking, cleaning, childcare), volunteer work, and informal economy transactions are not counted, even though they create real value. GDP would increase if everyone hired others to do what they currently do themselves.
  • Leisure not counted: GDP rises if people work more hours, but working more means less leisure — a cost that GDP ignores.
  • Environmental quality: Pollution and resource depletion reduce well-being but may actually increase GDP (cleaning up pollution counts as production). GDP does not subtract environmental damage.
  • Distribution: GDP says nothing about how output is distributed. A country with the same GDP as another might have extreme inequality while the other distributes income broadly.
  • Crime and social problems: Higher crime leads to more spending on security and prisons — which increases GDP, though well-being falls.
  • Composition of output: $1 billion in weapons and $1 billion in education add equally to GDP, but have very different social effects.
GDP Is Not Happiness
GDP per capita correlates with living standards, but misses crucial dimensions. Alternative measures include the Human Development Index (HDI), the Genuine Progress Indicator (GPI), and Bhutan's Gross National Happiness index.

9 · Key Terms

GDP
Total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a given period.
GNP
Total market value of all final goods produced by a country's citizens, wherever produced.
Final Goods & Services
Goods produced for final use — not for further processing by another firm.
Intermediate Goods
Goods produced for use in further processing by another firm. Excluded from GDP to avoid double counting.
Value Added
Value of output minus cost of inputs at each production stage. Sum of value added = final sale price.
Expenditure Approach
GDP = C + I + G + (EX − IM). Sums spending by all sectors.
Income Approach
GDP via wages, rents, interest, profits. Equals the expenditure approach by definition.
Personal Consumption (C)
Household spending on durable goods, nondurables, and services. 68.5% of U.S. GDP (2014).
Gross Private Domestic Investment (I)
Nonresidential + residential investment + change in business inventories. 16.4% of GDP.
Depreciation
Fall in asset value each period due to wear or obsolescence.
Net Investment
Gross investment minus depreciation. Positive = capital stock growing.
Government Consumption (G)
Federal, state, and local purchases of final goods and services. Excludes transfer payments.
Net Exports (NX)
Exports minus imports. Negative when imports exceed exports (trade deficit).
National Income
Total income earned by a country's factors of production. ≈ GNP − depreciation.
Personal Income
Total income received by households (before personal income taxes).
Disposable Personal Income
Personal income minus personal income taxes. What households have to spend or save.
Nominal GDP
GDP measured in current-year prices. Mixes price and output changes.
Real GDP
Nominal GDP adjusted for price changes. Measures true production growth.
GDP Deflator
(Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100. Measures overall price level across all final goods.
Personal Saving Rate
Personal saving / disposable personal income. U.S. rate was 4.8% in 2014.
