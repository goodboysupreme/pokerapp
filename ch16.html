Externalities, Public Goods & Common Resources

How markets fail when third-party costs or benefits go unpriced — and what governments can do about it.

1 · Externalities and Environmental Economics

An externality exists when the actions or decisions of one person or group impose a cost or bestow a benefit on second or third parties. Externalities are also called spillovers or neighborhood effects. When decision makers fail to consider these social costs and benefits, inefficient decisions result.

Externalities are pervasive: air, water, and noise pollution; traffic congestion; secondhand cigarette smoke; global warming; and acid rain are all examples. The study of externalities is the core concern of environmental economics.

Key Insight
The central problem with externalities: some costs (or benefits) are external to the decision maker's private calculation, so the market equilibrium diverges from the socially optimal outcome.

2 · Negative Externalities and Overproduction

When a firm dumps wastewater into a river or emits carbon while producing steel, it imposes costs on society that are not reflected in its private cost calculation. The marginal social cost (MSC) is the true total cost to society of producing one more unit — the sum of private marginal cost (MC) and the marginal external cost.

Relationship
MSC = MC + Marginal External Cost
When there are no externalities, MSC = MC and private output is efficient.

Without being charged for external costs, a firm produces at \(q^*\) where \(P = MC\), and the industry produces \(Q^*\). But the socially optimal output is \(Q^{**}\) where \(P = MSC\). The industry overproduces by \(Q^* - Q^{**}\). The deadweight loss (triangle ABC in the diagram below) represents the efficiency loss from overproduction.

Figure 16.1 — Negative Externality: Overproduction
Without internalising external costs, the industry produces Q* instead of the socially optimal Q**. The shaded triangle is deadweight loss.

3 · The Optimal Level of Pollution

Contrary to instinct, the socially optimal level of pollution is not zero. Every unit of pollution has a marginal social cost (MSC) — health losses, lost recreation, ecosystem damage — and a marginal social benefit of pollution (MSB) — the value of the resources saved by not cleaning it up. Eliminating the last unit of carbon emissions might mean shutting down all factories; that sacrifice is enormous.

The optimal emission level \(Q^*\) is where \(MSB = MSC\): society uses its environment most efficiently, equating the value of the last unit of pollution to the harm it causes.

Figure 16.2 — Socially Optimal Pollution Level
At Q*, marginal social benefit of pollution equals marginal social cost. This is the efficient emission level — not zero.
Why not zero pollution?
Eliminating the last unit of pollution can be prohibitively costly (shutting down entire industries). The MSB curve captures this avoided cost. Zero emissions would mean MSB > MSC — society is over-cleaning.

4 · Internalizing Externalities

Four policy mechanisms can force decision makers to account for external costs:

Private Bargaining — The Coase Theorem

Ronald Coase (1960, Nobel 1991) showed that under certain conditions, private parties can reach the efficient outcome without government intervention, regardless of how property rights are initially assigned. The key is that both parties bargain until the marginal benefit of the externality equals the marginal cost to the victim.

Coase Theorem
When property rights are clear and transaction costs are low, private bargaining will achieve the efficient outcome regardless of initial rights assignment.
Conditions for Coase
(1) Clear property rights, (2) low/zero transaction costs, (3) few parties involved. Fails with large groups due to free-rider problems.

Environmental Standards

Governments set direct rules (e.g., the EPA, Clean Air Act) on allowable emissions. Standards are easy to enforce but often inefficient: they force uniform cuts regardless of each firm's abatement cost, resulting in higher total costs than necessary.

Pigouvian Taxes and Subsidies

A per-unit tax equal to the marginal external cost forces the firm to face MSC directly, shifting its supply curve up to S' and reducing output to the social optimum. This is called a Pigouvian tax (after A.C. Pigou).

Advantage over standards: Taxes allow firms to choose their own abatement technology. Firms with cheap abatement cut more; those with expensive abatement cut less. Total emissions target is reached at minimum cost.

For positive externalities, subsidies increase output toward the social optimum.

Cap-and-Trade (Tradeable Emissions Permits)

The government sets a total cap on emissions and issues permits. Firms may trade permits in a market. Firms that can cut emissions cheaply do so and sell their excess permits. Firms with high abatement costs buy permits instead. Result: the target emission level is achieved at minimum total cost — identical to the tax outcome but using a market mechanism.

Example — Cap-and-Trade Arithmetic
Two firms each emit 5 units. Government caps total at 4 (each gets 2 permits). Firm A can abate cheaply ($5/unit first, $7/unit second, $12/unit third). Firm B abates expensively ($15, $19, $23/unit). Under a standard, each cuts 3 units: total abatement cost = $21 + $57 = $78. Under cap-and-trade, Firm A cuts to 1 unit (sells 1 permit to B). Firm B cuts to 3 units. If permit price = $12: Firm A spends $12 extra on abatement, earns $12 from permit sale. Firm B saves $23 by buying instead of abating. Total system cost is lower.

5 · Positive Externalities and Underproduction

When an activity generates external benefits to third parties, the marginal social benefit (MSB) exceeds the private benefit. Examples:

  • LoJack: Installing a theft-tracking device benefits not just the owner but all nearby car owners (deterrence). Buyers capture only ~10% of the value. Result: too few LoJacks are bought.
  • Vaccination: Immunising one person reduces disease transmission for everyone. Private benefit understates social benefit → underprovision.
  • Education: An educated workforce creates spillovers (lower crime, innovation). Society subsidises education partly for this reason.

Without intervention, the market underproduces goods with positive externalities. The solution is subsidies equal to the marginal external benefit, shifting demand (from the firm's perspective) up to the MSB curve.

6 · Public Goods

A public good has two defining properties that distinguish it from private goods:

Nonrival in Consumption
My consumption does not diminish your ability to consume it. National defense protects all citizens simultaneously. Contrast with a hamburger (private good): if I eat it, you cannot.
Nonexcludable
Once produced, no one can be excluded from benefiting. You cannot charge people individually for national defense.
Free-Rider Problem
Because you get the benefit whether or not you pay, rational individuals have no incentive to contribute voluntarily. Private production fails.
Drop-in-the-Bucket Problem
Any single person's contribution is negligible relative to the total cost. This reinforces the incentive not to contribute.
Public ≠ Government-Produced
A good is "public" because of its characteristics (nonrival + nonexcludable), not because the government provides it. A government could hand out free hamburgers — they remain private goods.

Public Provision of Public Goods

Because private markets fail to provide public goods (no one will pay voluntarily; no firm can profit), government must step in. The demand for a public good is found by vertically summing individual willingness-to-pay curves (unlike private goods where demand curves are horizontally summed), because all individuals consume the same quantity simultaneously.

Optimal Provision

For a public good, the socially optimal quantity is where the sum of all individuals' marginal willingness to pay equals the marginal cost of provision: \[ \sum_i MB_i = MC \] If person A values unit \(X_1\) at $6 and person B values it at $3, society values it at $9. Provision is optimal up to the quantity where this sum equals MC.

7 · Common Resources

Common resources (open-access resources) are rival but nonexcludable. Fish in the ocean, public grazing land, and groundwater are examples. Because no one can be excluded, individuals overuse them — each user captures the full benefit but shares the cost of depletion.

This is the Tragedy of the Commons (Garrett Hardin, 1968): individually rational behaviour leads to collective overexploitation and eventual destruction of the resource.

The Four Good Types
ExcludableNonexcludable
RivalPrivate goods (food, cars)Common resources (fish, groundwater)
NonrivalClub goods (Netflix, toll roads)Public goods (defense, lighthouses)

Solutions: privatisation (assign property rights), government regulation (fishing quotas), or community management (Elinor Ostrom's work on self-governing commons, Nobel 2009).

8 · Key Terms

Externality
Cost or benefit imposed on third parties not involved in a transaction.
Marginal Social Cost (MSC)
Total cost to society of one more unit of output = MC + marginal external cost.
Marginal Social Benefit of Pollution
Benefit to society from one more unit of pollution — the cost saved by not abating.
Coase Theorem
Private bargaining achieves efficient outcome when rights are clear and transaction costs are low.
Injunction
Court order forbidding damage-producing behaviour.
Liability Rules
Laws requiring a party to compensate for damages caused.
Cap-and-Trade
Government sets emission cap; permits are tradeable so reductions happen where cheapest.
Public Good
Nonrival in consumption and nonexcludable. Markets underprovide or fail entirely.
Nonrival
A's consumption does not reduce B's — all can consume simultaneously.
Nonexcludable
Once produced, no one can be excluded from consuming the good.
Free-Rider Problem
Rational incentive to enjoy a public good without paying for it.
Drop-in-the-Bucket
One person's contribution is negligible; undermines voluntary provision.
Common Resources
Rival but nonexcludable — leads to overuse (Tragedy of the Commons).
