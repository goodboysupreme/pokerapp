1 · The Three Primary Concerns of Macroeconomics

Macroeconomics deals with the economy as a whole — total national income, aggregate consumption and investment, and the overall price level — in contrast to microeconomics, which examines individual industries and decision-making units (firms and households).

A key reason macroeconomics emerged as a distinct field is sticky prices: some prices do not adjust rapidly to clear markets. In a single apple market, a surplus quickly drives price down. But in the aggregate labour market, wages can remain above market-clearing levels, producing persistent unemployment — a puzzle that classical microeconomic models could not explain.

Government policy makers pursue three macroeconomic goals — which often conflict:

Output Growth
Sustained increase in real aggregate output (real GDP). The economy experiences short-run ups and downs — the business cycle — around a long-run growth trend.
Low Unemployment
The unemployment rate (% of labour force unemployed) is a key indicator of economic health. Some positive unemployment is inevitable in a dynamic economy (frictional, structural).
Low Inflation
Inflation = increase in overall price level. Deflation = decrease. Both prolonged inflation and deflation are harmful. Hyperinflation (Bolivia 1984–85: egg price rose from 3,000 to 10,000 pesos in a week) can destabilise governments.
Goal Conflicts
Policies that reduce unemployment (stimulating demand) can push up inflation. Policies that lower inflation (contractionary) can increase unemployment. Understanding these trade-offs is central to macroeconomics.

2 · Business Cycle: Peaks, Troughs, Recessions

Economies don't grow smoothly — they experience business cycles: short-term fluctuations in aggregate output around a long-run trend.

Aggregate Output
Total quantity of goods and services produced in an economy in a given period. The main measure of how the economy is doing.
Recession
Aggregate output declines for two consecutive quarters. Firms lay off workers; unemployment rises.
Depression
Prolonged and deep recession. The U.S. had one in the 1930s.
Expansion / Boom
Period from trough to peak. Output and employment grow.
Contraction / Slump
Period from peak to trough. Output and employment fall.
Peak
Highest point of the business cycle before a contraction begins.
Trough
Lowest point of the business cycle before expansion begins.
Figure 20.1 — A Typical Business Cycle
At point A (early expansion), output is growing (positive rate of change) but still at a low level. At point B (early contraction), output is falling but still at a high level.
Level vs. Rate of Change
If the economy just left a trough (point A), it is growing (positive rate of change) but its level is still low. If it just turned from a peak (point B), output is falling but the level remains high. Don't confuse whether output is high with whether it's rising.

U.S. recessions since WWII: 1946, 1954, 1958, 1974–75, 1980–82, 1990–91, 2001, and 2008–09. Cycles before WWII were more extreme; the 2008–09 recovery was unusually slow.

3 · Components of the Macroeconomy

The macroeconomy has four broad groups of participants:

  • Households — earn income, spend on goods/services, save, pay taxes
  • Firms — produce output, pay wages, invest, pay taxes
  • Government — collects taxes, purchases goods/services, makes transfer payments, sets policy
  • Rest of the World — buys exports, sells imports

The private sector = households + firms. The public sector = government. The foreign sector = rest of the world.

4 · The Circular Flow Diagram

The circular flow diagram shows the flows of income and payments among the four groups. Key principle: every expenditure is someone else's receipt.

Figure 20.3 — Circular Flow of Payments
Every payment by one sector is income for another. Household saving and taxes are "leakages" from the circular flow; government spending and investment are "injections."
Transfer Payments
Transfer payments (Social Security, veterans' benefits, welfare) are cash payments by government to people who supply no goods or services in return. They appear in the circular flow as flows from government to households but are NOT included in GDP (not payments for current production).

5 · The Three Market Arenas

Goods-and-Services Market
Households, government, and firms buy final goods and services. Firms supply; households, government, and the rest of the world demand. Includes exports and imports.
Labour Market
Firms and government buy labour from households. Households supply labour. Labour is increasingly international (migrant workers, call centres abroad).
Money (Financial) Market
Households buy stocks and bonds from firms; lend to firms, government, and each other. Coordinated by banks, insurance companies, and other financial institutions. Key variable: the interest rate.

Key Financial Instruments

  • Treasury bonds/notes/bills: Promissory notes issued by the federal government when borrowing.
  • Corporate bonds: Promissory notes issued by corporations.
  • Shares of stock: Give the holder part ownership in a firm — rights to a share of profits (dividends) and capital gains if the stock price rises.

6 · Government: Fiscal and Monetary Policy

Fiscal Policy
Government decisions about taxing and spending. Expansionary = cut taxes or increase spending. Contractionary = raise taxes or cut spending. Set by Congress and the President.
Monetary Policy
Tools used by the Federal Reserve (the Fed) to control short-term interest rates. The Fed is the U.S. central bank. Chair has been called the second most powerful person in the U.S. after the President.

Both fiscal and monetary policy were actively used during the 2008–09 recession. The Fed played an especially expanded role in that crisis compared to previous recessions.

7 · Brief History of Macroeconomics (1929–1970)

The Great Depression (1929–1939)

The defining event that created macroeconomics as a field. By 1933, 13 million of 51 million workers were unemployed (25%+). U.S. output fell 27% from 1929 to 1933. Classical models predicted wages would fall, increasing labour demand and clearing the market. But unemployment persisted for nearly a decade. Classical models failed.

Keynes and The General Theory (1936)

John Maynard Keynes published The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money — one of the most important works in economics. Key arguments:

  • Employment is determined not only by prices and wages but by the level of aggregate demand (total spending).
  • Wages may be sticky downward — markets don't always self-correct quickly.
  • Government can and should intervene: when private demand is low, fiscal stimulus can lift the economy out of recession.

Post-WWII Acceptance and Fine-Tuning (1950s–60s)

Keynesian views gained influence. The Employment Act of 1946 committed the U.S. government to preventing large declines in output and employment and established the President's Council of Economic Advisers. By the 1960s, Walter Heller popularised the idea of fine-tuning — actively using fiscal and monetary policy to keep the economy on a smooth growth path.

8 · The U.S. Economy Since 1970

Stagflation (1970s)

The 1970s brought a challenge to Keynesian theory: stagflation — high unemployment and high inflation simultaneously. Classical Keynesian models had not predicted this could happen (inflation was supposed to accompany low unemployment). The 1973 and 1979 oil shocks (OPEC) triggered massive supply-side cost increases, raising prices while reducing output.

Supply-Side Economics (1980s)

The Reagan administration emphasised cutting taxes — particularly on high earners — and reducing regulation to stimulate the supply side of the economy (production capacity, incentives to work and invest). Some economists argued the economy had been suffering from demand management failures and excessive government intervention.

New Classical Economics

Starting in the 1970s, economists like Robert Lucas argued that households and firms form rational expectations — they anticipate policy and adjust immediately, making systematic government intervention ineffective. This challenged the Keynesian consensus.

Ongoing Debate
Macroeconomics has no single dominant theory. Keynesian, monetarist, new classical, and new Keynesian schools each offer insights. Real-world policy blends all of them depending on circumstances.

9 · Key Terms

Macroeconomics
Study of the economy as a whole — total income, aggregate output, overall prices.
Microeconomics
Study of individual industries, firms, and households.
Sticky Prices
Prices that don't adjust rapidly enough to clear markets; a key reason for unemployment.
Aggregate Output
Total quantity of goods and services produced in an economy in a given period.
Business Cycle
Short-term ups and downs in aggregate output around a long-run trend.
Recession
Aggregate output declines for two consecutive quarters.
Depression
Prolonged and deep recession.
Expansion / Boom
Trough to peak: output and employment grow.
Inflation
Increase in the overall price level.
Hyperinflation
Very rapid increase in the overall price level.
Deflation
Decrease in the overall price level.
Circular Flow
Diagram showing flows of payments among households, firms, government, and the rest of the world.
Transfer Payments
Government payments (Social Security, welfare) not in exchange for goods or services.
Fiscal Policy
Government decisions about taxing and spending.
Monetary Policy
Federal Reserve tools to control short-term interest rates.
Great Depression
1929–1939 economic catastrophe that gave birth to macroeconomics.
Aggregate Demand
Total spending in the economy on final goods and services.
Stagflation
Simultaneous high inflation and high unemployment (1970s).
Fine-Tuning
1960s idea that government could continuously adjust policy to keep the economy stable.
